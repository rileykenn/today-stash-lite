How to Increase Foot Traffic to Your Café in Australia: A Practical Guide for Café Owners
Every café owner in Australia knows the pattern. Friday morning is chaos — every table full, a queue out the door, staff flat out. Then Monday rolls around and you're watching three tables turn over in four hours while your team stands around folding napkins.
The challenge isn't making your café popular. You've already done that. The challenge is spreading that popularity more evenly across the week — filling the dead patches without undermining the busy ones, and doing it in a way that's sustainable for your margins and your team.
This guide is written specifically for Australian café operators — from inner-city espresso bars to regional main street institutions — on exactly how to increase foot traffic in the periods that matter most.

Understand Your Quiet Periods Before You Do Anything Else
The single biggest mistake café owners make when trying to increase foot traffic is running promotions at the wrong time. A "buy one get one free" offer that fires on Saturday morning doesn't help your business — it just discounts revenue you were already going to make.
Before you launch any promotion or marketing campaign, pull your sales data and map your foot traffic by day and time. Most POS systems — Square, Lightspeed, Kounta — will show you this in a few clicks. What you're looking for is:
	•	Which days consistently underperform?
	•	Which time slots within those days are the quietest?
	•	Is the pattern seasonal, or consistent year-round?
For most Australian cafés, the answers look something like this: Monday and Tuesday are the quietest days, mid-afternoon (2–4pm) is the dead zone, and the shoulder between breakfast and lunch (10–11am) often underperforms. Your promotions should target those windows precisely — not your already-busy Saturday brunch.

The Most Effective Foot Traffic Strategies for Australian Cafés
1. Time-limited weekday offers via a local deal platform
The most direct way to fill quiet weekday periods is to give locals a compelling reason to visit on those specific days. A well-structured in-store offer — "Monday and Tuesday: free cake slice with any coffee" or "Weekday lunch: any sandwich + coffee for $16" — provides exactly that trigger.
The key is making the offer available through a platform where locals are actively browsing for things to do and places to eat. Today's Stash connects café customers with exactly these kinds of local, in-store offers — browsable by area, redeemable with a simple counter QR code.
The mechanics matter here. The offer needs to be:
	•	Time-specific — only available on your quiet days, not seven days a week
	•	Capped — you decide how many redemptions per day so you never get overwhelmed
	•	Margin-safe — the discount should only ever apply to capacity that would otherwise sit idle, not your peak trade
2. Google Business Profile — the most underused tool in café marketing
If you haven't claimed and fully optimised your Google Business Profile, do it today. It's free and it's the single highest-leverage thing a café can do for local discoverability.
When someone searches "café near me" or "best café in [your town]" on Google Maps, the businesses that appear prominently are the ones with complete, active Google Business Profiles. This means:
	•	Accurate trading hours (including holiday variations)
	•	High-quality photos of your space, food and coffee — updated regularly
	•	A description that includes your suburb/town name and what you're known for
	•	Active responses to every review, positive and negative
	•	Posts updated at least monthly with specials, new menu items or events
Many regional Australian cafés rank in the top three for local searches simply by maintaining an active, complete Google Business Profile while competitors neglect theirs. It costs nothing except ten minutes a week.
3. The loyalty mechanic that actually changes behaviour
Digital punch cards and loyalty programs work — but only if they're structured to drive the behaviour you actually want. Most café loyalty programs reward total spend, which means your most loyal customers already come in daily and get rewarded for behaviour they'd do anyway. That's great for retention, but it doesn't fill your quiet Tuesday afternoons.
A more targeted approach: offer a weekday-only multiplier. Visits Monday to Wednesday earn double stamps or double points. Weekend visits earn standard points. This directly incentivises customers to spread their visits across the week rather than concentrating them on weekends.
Apps like Stamp Me and Hey Loyalty support this kind of conditional logic and are used by thousands of Australian cafés.
4. Corporate and workplace accounts
If your café is within walking distance of offices, businesses, schools or any workplace with 10+ staff, a corporate account program can be a significant and consistent source of weekday foot traffic.
The model is simple: businesses set up an account with you and staff order through a tab or prepaid credit. Many cafés offer a modest group discount (10–15%) in exchange for the guaranteed volume and advance ordering that reduces waste.
Approach businesses directly — walk in, introduce yourself, leave a one-page summary of the arrangement. A single office of 20 people ordering daily coffees is worth $50,000–$70,000 in annual revenue and almost entirely fills weekday morning quiet periods.
5. Local community presence — not just social media posting
Most café owners know they should be more active on Instagram. Few have the time or inclination to do it consistently, and frankly, organic social media reach for local businesses has declined significantly in the past five years.
What works better is genuine community presence:
	•	Sponsor a local sporting club or school event — your logo on their jersey or their program, your café as the go-to post-game coffee stop. A $500–$1,000 annual sponsorship often delivers more foot traffic than $500 of Instagram ads.
	•	Partner with a complementary local business — the bookshop next door, the florist down the street, the yoga studio around the corner. Cross-promotions ("show your receipt from X, get a free coffee upgrade at Y") cost nothing and tap into each other's loyal customer bases.
	•	Host a recurring local event — a monthly book club, a weekly trivia morning, a Saturday morning drawing group. Events fill specific time slots reliably and create habitual visit patterns.
6. Seasonal and event-tied promotions
Australia's regional calendar is full of hooks — the local show, the football season, school holidays, Anzac Day, Mother's Day, the Melbourne Cup. Each of these is an opportunity to run a short, themed campaign that gives customers a timely reason to visit.
The key is preparing these campaigns in advance rather than scrambling the week before. Map out your promotional calendar for the year at the start of each year, identify the five or six moments most relevant to your specific community, and build your campaigns around those anchors.

What to Avoid When Trying to Increase Café Foot Traffic
Avoid blanket discounting. "10% off all week, every week" devalues your product and trains customers to wait for discounts rather than paying your real prices. Reserve discounts for targeted, time-limited situations with a clear strategic purpose.
Avoid platforms that take a large cut of your revenue. Some daily deal platforms operate on a 50/50 split model — if you sell a $20 voucher, you receive $10 but still deliver the full experience. At café margins, this is often a loss-making exercise. Always understand the fee structure before committing.
Avoid promotions that create staffing nightmares. An offer that brings in 80 people to a café staffed for 30 creates a terrible experience for everyone. Cap your redemptions and only promote capacity you can actually handle gracefully.
Avoid being inconsistent. A promotional offer that runs for two weeks and then disappears builds no habit. The most effective café promotions run for a defined season or quarter so that customers can plan around them and tell their friends.
